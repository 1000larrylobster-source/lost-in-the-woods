# Devlog 000: Why this game

**Date: 2026-07-06**
**Status: design phase, zero code**

## Where the idea came from

I work in low-voltage systems and access control. Cameras, sensors, radios, controllers, the wiring that makes a building aware of itself. That is the day job and it has been for years.

Somewhere along the way I noticed that every game tech tree is fake. You research "Advanced Optics" and a bar fills up. Nobody solders anything. Nobody does the battery math. Nobody stands on a ridge with a directional antenna trying to get one more mile of link out of a node. The most interesting part of the hardware, the part where physics pushes back, gets abstracted into a progress bar.

Meanwhile the real stuff got cheap. A LoRa mesh node is a weekend and pocket money. A Pi Zero flies. Hobbyists run thermal cameras. The hardware in my tech tree is not speculative, it is a shopping list.

The idea clicked as one sentence: **you start as the lost hiker, and you end as the network that finds lost hikers.** Get found, then become the finder. Search-and-rescue meets base-builder, and the base is radio coverage stretched over a mountain.

The fire angle came right behind it. A sensor grid that watches for lost people is the same grid that catches a fire while it is still one tree. Same hardware, higher stakes, natural late game.

## Why design it in public

I run my business the same way: build the machine in the open, show what works and what does not. This repo is the design process, not a trailer. Wrong calls will sit in the history where anyone can see them. That is a feature.

Also practical: I know the hardware side cold, but this is my first game design. People who know game feel, engines, and scope better than me can catch mistakes early. Issues are open.

## What gets decided next

Devlog 001 answers exactly one question: **engine choice.** Godot is the front-runner and I want it committed or killed, in writing, with reasons. The 2D vs 3D call likely falls out of the same decision.
