# devlog 007 — the night the Range got built

The instruction was simple and it wasn't small: *go through the entire checklist, top
to bottom. Ten maps. Make it worth paying for. When you think you're done, you're not.*

So: one night, one file, the suite run green in a real browser after every increment.
Where it started: one valley, 44 tests. Where it ended: **the Range — ten regions,
57 tests, v1.0.**

**The architecture came first.** Every region is this same engine under a different
sky — a config block: terrain scale, tree density, night length, fire temper, a full
palette. Blackpine Lake really is darker, denser, and its nights really are longer.
The relay pad joined the map with a third set of component sites by the pass: meet
your coverage goal, raise the relay, and cross. Your saves are per-region; the Range
remembers who you've recruited, what you've finished, and whether you've ever crossed
with *not one tree burned* (that's worth something).

**Then the year happened to the fire.** The wind lives now — it picks headings with
intent and gusts in pulses that hurry every burning cell; the arrow leans so you can
read it coming. On the lake, fires learned to start after dark, and the counter is a
real part: the NoIR camera, night eyes that trade a little daylight reach. The meadow
got its herd — elk that trip the drones as MOVING HEAT, honest false positives you
confirm on foot, and if you come at them from downwind, dead slow, you can close to
knife range and eat for two days. Storms ground your drones and take your antennas
exactly when the rain has made the forest safest. Snow buries panels quiet as guilt.
Drought throws embers over your firebreaks. Dry lightning walks in threes. And in the
Old Burn, almost nothing is new — you strip ghost towers left by the crew before, and
when that runs out, you pull the panel off your own live tower and carry the hole in
your coverage on your back.

**The finale is made of the nine lessons before it.** Fire season on the Crown: waves
of ignitions, three to five at once — *triage* — for three dawns. Survive them and the
rain comes, the way it always does: late.

**And the quiet things.** A title screen that knows your progress. A card when you
enter a region and a card when you hold it. Sounds for every verb — each component
mounts with its own clank. Stars that come out with the dark. Clouds on the wind. And
seven things hidden that no map will ever mark: a first boot that says *hello, world*;
a sapling, twenty days on, in the first ground you ever lost; a badge reader on a
bunker door that chirps exactly the way real ones do, because the person this game is
built with installs them for a living; a box labeled KVM that means you never hike a
ridge for a frozen node again; a cabin with one warm window, a red lobster on the
shelf, and a note about an eleventh map; and a constellation you can only resolve
from dark high ground, with one dim red star pulsing like a status LED.

57/57. One file. Worth paying for is a claim buyers get to judge — but tonight it
stopped being an aspiration and became a checklist with everything ticked.
